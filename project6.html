VTU App

UI/UX Design, Mobile-first Front-end Development

Overview

VTU App is a mobile-first web application built to simplify airtime and data purchases for users across Nigeria. I led the UI/UX design, developed the responsive front-end, and ensured a seamless, intuitive user experience from onboarding to checkout.

User Research

Method: Conducted surveys and usability interviews with 20+ users who regularly purchase airtime/data online.

Findings: Users desired fast load times, minimal form fields, and secure, hassle-free payment flow. Many cited frustration with existing clunky platforms.

Problem Statement

How might we design a fast, clean, and secure platform that allows users to recharge airtime and data with minimal effort and high trust?

Design Process

Personas: Created personas for “The Busy Professional” and “The Student on a Budget.”

Wireframes: Designed low to mid-fidelity wireframes in Figma with a focus on clarity and one-hand usability on mobile.

Prototyping: Developed high-fidelity interactive prototypes for user testing and client presentation.

Visual Design: Used vibrant gradients, soft shadows, and icons to enhance usability and trust.

Development

Initial Build: Crafted a mobile-first responsive layout using HTML5, CSS3, and minimal JavaScript.

Performance: Optimized assets and implemented lazy loading for faster load times, especially on 3G connections.

Security: Integrated secure form handling and input validation best practices in collaboration with backend devs.

Challenges

Challenge: Designing for a wide range of Android device sizes while maintaining visual consistency.

Solution: Adopted a flexible grid system and tested on multiple screen resolutions to ensure fluid scaling and accessibility.

Results

User Impact: Users reported high satisfaction with the simplicity and speed. Conversion rate increased by 40% after redesign.

Client Feedback: “This app works beautifully on mobile. Our users finally enjoy topping up without stress.”

Testimonial

“This project reinforced the importance of designing for real-world constraints — like poor networks and small screens.”
